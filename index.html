Journey Begins

Antioch marks the pivotal moment when Paul's missionary calling became reality. This wasn't a human decision‚Äîthe Holy Spirit specifically commanded the church to send Paul and Barnabas on their mission. After corporate prayer and fasting, the church commissioned them for the work that would change the Roman world.

This city had already proven its significance in early Christianity. It was in Antioch that believers were first called "Christians" (Acts 11:26). The church here was diverse, including both Jews and Gentiles, and would become the launching pad for the gospel to reach the ends of the earth.

The City

Antioch on the Orontes (modern-day Antakya, Turkey) was one of the greatest cities of the ancient world. Founded in 300 BC by Seleucus I Nicator, it became the capital of the Seleucid Empire. By Paul's time, it was the third-largest city in the Roman Empire, after Rome and Alexandria, with a population exceeding 500,000.

The city was built on both sides of the Orontes River, with a main street that stretched four miles through the city center. This colonnaded avenue was lined with shops, markets, and public buildings. The city was famous for its beauty, its hippodrome, its theater, and the magnificent sanctuary of Daphne‚Äîa suburb renowned for its temples and pleasure gardens.

üí° Why Antioch Was Strategic

Antioch sat at the crossroads of major trade routes connecting the Mediterranean to Asia. Its port city, Seleucia, gave direct access to Cyprus and the broader Mediterranean world. This made it the perfect launching point for missionary journeys‚Äîboth geographically and spiritually.

Political Climate

In AD 46, the Roman Emperor was Claudius (AD 41-54). The empire was experiencing relative peace under the Pax Romana, which made travel safer and communication easier‚Äîdivine providence for spreading the gospel. Syria was an imperial province governed directly by Rome, making Antioch an important administrative center.

However, tensions existed. Claudius had expelled Jews from Rome around AD 49 (mentioned in Acts 18:2), showing the complex relationship between Judaism and Roman authority. The church in Antioch existed in this delicate balance between Jewish heritage and Gentile inclusion.

üåç What Was Happening in AD 46

AD 35-46: Church Growth

After persecution in Jerusalem (Acts 8), believers scattered to Antioch and began preaching to Greeks. The church experienced explosive growth, requiring Barnabas to recruit Paul from Tarsus to help teach (Acts 11:19-26).

AD 44: James Martyred

Just two years before Paul's departure, James the brother of John was executed by Herod Agrippa I in Jerusalem (Acts 12:2). Peter was imprisoned but miraculously escaped. Persecution was intensifying.

AD 46: Prophets Visit

The prophet Agabus had recently come from Jerusalem to Antioch and predicted a great famine (Acts 11:28). The Antioch church sent relief to Jerusalem through Barnabas and Paul‚Äîdemonstrating the partnership between these two churches.

AD 46: Time of Calling

After returning from their relief mission to Jerusalem, Paul and Barnabas were back in Antioch when the Holy Spirit called them to mission work. The church had grown mature enough to send out its best teachers.

Cultural Context

Antioch was a melting pot of cultures‚ÄîGreek, Roman, Syrian, and Jewish. Greek was the common language, but Aramaic and Latin were also spoken. The city was known for both its sophistication and its moral decadence. The sanctuary of Daphne was notorious for ritual prostitution and debauchery.

Yet into this morally complex environment, the gospel had taken root. The church at Antioch represented a radical new community where "there is neither Jew nor Gentile, slave nor free" (Galatians 3:28)‚Äîa revolutionary concept in the stratified society of the Roman Empire.

‚úùÔ∏è Paul's Activities in Antioch

Before the Journey

Paul had already spent considerable time in Antioch before his missionary calling. Barnabas brought him from Tarsus around AD 44-45 (Acts 11:25-26). For a whole year, they taught the growing church. This was Paul's training ground‚Äîlearning to teach both Jews and Gentiles, working alongside other leaders, and seeing the power of a diverse Christian community.

The Commissioning

The sending of Paul and Barnabas was a deliberate, Spirit-led process. The church was engaged in worship and fasting‚Äîseeking God's direction. When the Holy Spirit spoke (possibly through one of the prophets present), the instruction was clear: set apart these two men for a special work.

The laying on of hands symbolized the church's blessing, authority, and partnership in the mission. This wasn't Paul going rogue‚Äîhe went with the full backing of the Antioch church, which would become his home base for all three missionary journeys.

üí° Significance of Church Sending

Paul wasn't a lone ranger missionary. He was sent by a local church, accountable to that church, and would return to report to them (Acts 14:26-27). This established the biblical pattern for missions‚Äîlocal churches sending, supporting, and receiving missionaries.

Companions from Antioch

Barnabas - A Levite from Cyprus, he was Paul's mentor and partner. His name means "son of encouragement," and he lived up to it by vouching for Paul when others doubted his conversion.

John Mark - Though not mentioned in Acts 13:1-3, John Mark (later author of Mark's Gospel) accompanied them as a helper. His mother's house in Jerusalem was a meeting place for the early church.

Temple of Bel, Palmyra
While not in Antioch itself, Palmyra was part of the same Syrian region. This magnificent temple (tragically destroyed by ISIS in 2015) represented the architectural grandeur of the era when Paul began his journeys.
Orontes River
The Orontes River (modern Asi River) that flowed through the heart of ancient Antioch, providing water and commerce to the great city where Paul's journey began.
St. Peter's Cave Church
One of the oldest Christian churches, traditionally where early Christians met secretly in Antioch to worship and hear the apostles teach. This cave witnessed the birth of the church that sent Paul.
Ancient Roman Road
Roman road from ancient Syria - Paul would have traveled well-maintained roads like this from Antioch to the port of Seleucia, about 15 miles away, beginning his first journey.

Modern Antakya

Today, the ancient city is known as Antakya in southern Turkey, near the Syrian border. While much of ancient Antioch lies buried beneath the modern city, several significant archaeological remains have been discovered, including impressive mosaics, city walls, and the traditional Cave Church of St. Peter.

Sadly, a devastating earthquake in February 2023 caused significant damage to Antakya, including the historic Cave Church of St. Peter. The city is being rebuilt, just as the spiritual legacy of the Antioch church continues to build Christ's kingdom worldwide.

üîó Connection to Today

Lessons from Antioch

1. The Power of a Sending Church: The Antioch church didn't hoard its best leaders‚Äîit sent them. They understood that blessing comes through giving.

2. Diversity is Strength: The leadership team at Antioch included people from different backgrounds‚ÄîAfrican (Simeon Niger), North African (Lucius of Cyrene), Jewish (Paul, Barnabas), and even someone from Herod's court. This diversity reflected God's kingdom.

3. Worship Leads to Mission: The missionary calling came during worship and fasting‚Äînot during a strategy meeting. When we seek God's face, He shows us His heart for the lost.

4. Partnership in Gospel Work: Paul didn't go alone. The church sent him with Barnabas, provided support, and welcomed him back. Mission is a team effort.
